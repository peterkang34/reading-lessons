Lessons for Holdcos

What eight unconventional CEOs can teach anyone building a portfolio of businesses. Capital allocation, M&A discipline, HQ design, and the math of per-share value.

Prepared by Peter Kang
The Connection

A playbook for holdco builders

The Outsiders profiles eight CEOs of public companies who delivered exceptional shareholder returns over twenty-plus year tenures. Capital Cities, Teledyne, General Dynamics, Berkshire, the Washington Post, TCI, General Cinema, and Ralston Purina were not the same kind of business as a modern holdco. But the operating system was the same: lean headquarters, decentralized operating units, disciplined deployment of cash flow, and a relentless focus on returns per share.

These are the lessons a holdco builder can pull directly from the book. They are not about technique. They are about how the person at the top thinks about the job.

The Lessons

What the outsiders teach holdco builders

Twelve lessons on capital allocation, portfolio design, and building a holdco that compounds for decades.

Lesson 01
Capital allocation is the job, not a side function
“The allocation process should be CEO led, not delegated to finance or business development personnel.”
The Outsiders

The single most useful frame in the book is that a holdco CEO has two jobs: run the existing operations efficiently and decide where the cash goes. Most of the time and energy at the top should go to the second one. Operating excellence at the portfolio level is delegated. Capital is not.

That means the holdco CEO sits with the model, picks the hurdle rate, ranks the alternatives, and signs off on every meaningful deployment. Outsourcing this to a corporate development team or a CFO is the single most common reason holdcos drift toward mediocre returns. The math is simple. The discipline of doing it personally is what is rare.

Lesson 02
Set a hurdle rate and refuse to drift
“Acquisitions needed to earn a minimum 11 percent cash return without leverage over a ten-year holding period. Very few deals passed through this screen. The company’s whole acquisition ethos was to wait for just the right deal.”
on Katharine Graham

Murphy used a double-digit after-tax return over ten years, unleveraged. Graham used 11% cash return over ten years. Stiritz used the share repurchase return as the floor. Singleton refused to pay more than 12x earnings.

Pick a number. Write it down. Apply it to every deal. The hurdle becomes a filter that does most of the work for you, because it forces a no on the deals that look good in a deck but do not pass arithmetic. Murphy never won an auction. His bids landed at 60-70% of the eventual price. That is what discipline looks like in practice.

Holdcos that move the hurdle to fit the deal end up with portfolios that look full but compound poorly. The hurdle exists to prevent that.

Lesson 03
Treat buybacks as a hurdle, not a press release
“The hurdle we always used for investment decisions was the share repurchase return. If an acquisition, with some certainty, could beat that return, it was worth doing.”
Pat Mulcahy on Bill Stiritz

Singleton bought back 90% of Teledyne’s shares across eight tender offers. The lesson for a holdco is not the action itself. It is the way buybacks set the comparison for everything else.

If your stock or the equivalent equity in your holdco can be repurchased at a 15% IRR, then any acquisition has to clear that bar with margin. Otherwise you are destroying value relative to the simpler alternative. This frame eliminates a lot of bad deals at the second meeting.

The implementation matters. Use the suction hose, not the straw. Big repurchases when the equity is cheap. Skip them when it is expensive. Most public buyback programs are window dressing. The Outsiders treated theirs as the cleanest possible capital deployment.

Lesson 04
Be wary of the word “strategic”
“Be very wary of the adjective strategic—it is often corporate code for low returns.”
The Outsiders

The most expensive deals in any holdco’s history are the ones justified by synergy, optionality, or platform value rather than by IRR. Thorndike notices this pattern across all eight CEOs. They were suspicious of any pitch that needed adjectives instead of numbers.

If a deal requires you to use words like strategic, transformational, or category-defining to clear the math, the math probably does not clear. Hold the line. The bakery example in the book is the right model: real numbers, conservative assumptions, an explicit return calculation, and the discipline to pass when the answer is no.

Lesson 05
HQ exists to allocate capital and carry culture
“We don’t believe in staff. Staff are people who second-guess people.”
John Malone, TCI

TCI ran 12 million subscribers with 17 people at corporate. Teledyne had fewer than 50 in a 40,000-person company. General Dynamics under Anders cut corporate staff by 80%. The pattern repeats across all eight.

The instinct as a holdco grows is to add functions: HR, legal, IT, marketing, business development. Each addition makes operating company leaders feel less responsible for their results and more managed. The Outsiders resisted this completely. HQ allocates capital, sets the hurdle, and carries the culture. Operating companies do everything else.

If you cannot say what each person at corporate does for shareholder returns, they should probably not be there.

Lesson 06
Decentralize because HQ does not have the answers
“There is a fundamental humility to decentralization, an admission that headquarters does not have all the answers and that much of the real value is created by local managers in the field.”
The Outsiders

Decentralization in the book is not a structural choice made for efficiency. It is a statement about where value gets created. Murphy gave Capital Cities station managers full P&L authority and the highest margins in the industry followed. Singleton broke Teledyne into its smallest units and put accountability on the unit GMs.

The holdco implication is clear. Operating company leaders should run their own businesses and own their own results. The holdco does not set strategy for them, does not approve hires below their direct reports, and does not insert itself into customer or product decisions. It approves capital, monitors results against the agreed metrics, and otherwise gets out of the way.

Lesson 07
Optimize for cash flow, not reported earnings
“Higher net income meant higher taxes, and he believed that the best strategy was to use all available tools to minimize reported earnings and taxes, and fund internal growth and acquisitions with pretax cash flow.”
on John Malone

Singleton invented the Teledyne return. Malone invented EBITDA. Both did it because reported earnings were not measuring the thing they actually cared about, which was cash that could be redeployed.

For a holdco, this means picking metrics that survive scrutiny over decades: free cash flow, return on tangible capital, and the cash actually available for the next acquisition or buyback. Operating company managers should be compensated on cash, not on accounting profit. Tax planning should be a permanent obsession, not a once-a-year exercise.

The math also implies that the holdco should be willing to sacrifice short-term reported earnings for long-term cash. Most public companies cannot do this. A private holdco can.

Lesson 08
Wait years for the right deal, then bet big
“Murphy was willing to wait a long time for an attractive acquisition. When he saw something that he liked, however, Murphy was prepared to make a very large bet. These acquisitions each represented 25 percent or more of the company’s market capitalization at the time.”
on Tom Murphy

The Outsider pattern on M&A is patience punctuated by boldness. Long stretches of small or no deals, then a single transaction that resets the company. Murphy bought ABC. Anders bought Cessna. Stiritz bought Energizer in three meetings.

Most holdcos do the opposite: a steady drumbeat of mid-sized deals at the going rate, none of which move the needle. The result is a portfolio that grows in absolute terms but does not compound per share.

The discipline is to keep saying no while the obvious deal forms, then to act decisively when it does. Stiritz’s Energizer process is the model. Books at 1pm, model at 4pm, bid at $1.4 billion. No bankers, no committee. The work had been done in advance, in the form of knowing exactly what they were looking for.

Lesson 09
Shrink, sell, and spin off when the math says so
“Most CEOs grade themselves on size and growth . . . very few really focus on shareholder returns.”
Bill Anders, General Dynamics

Anders sold half of General Dynamics in three years and returned the proceeds to shareholders. Stiritz spun off Energizer at 15% of company value. Buffett closed the textile mill that gave Berkshire its name. Malone spun off fourteen entities.

For a holdco, the lesson is structural. The portfolio is not a museum. Operating companies that no longer clear the hurdle should be sold, spun, or closed. The cash from a divestiture is often more valuable than the business it came from, especially when the equivalent capital can be deployed at higher returns elsewhere.

This is the hardest discipline in the book because most CEOs are emotionally attached to size. The Outsiders were not. When Anders was offered a fair price for his favorite business unit, he sold it.

Lesson 10
Use leverage for operating businesses, not for repurchases
“Always, we’ve taken the assets once we’ve paid them off and leveraged them again to buy other assets. Murphy never borrowed money to fund a share repurchase, preferring to utilize leverage for the purchase of operating businesses.”
on Tom Murphy

Murphy and Malone both used debt aggressively, but with a clear mental model. Debt was for buying operating businesses with predictable cash flows that could service the loan. The cash flow from the new acquisition paid down the debt, freeing up the asset to be releveraged for the next deal. Buybacks were funded from cash, not debt.

For a holdco, this is a useful guardrail. Acquisition debt has a self-amortizing logic when the underlying business throws off cash. Buyback debt does not. The same principle applies to growth capex versus distributions: debt for things that produce cash, equity for things that return it.

Malone’s bulkheading at TCI is the more advanced version: structure the debt so a default in one operating company does not propagate to the others.

Lesson 11
Build a board of owners with skin in the game
“Buffett believes that the best boards are composed of relatively small groups (Berkshire has twelve directors) of experienced businesspeople with large ownership stakes. (He requires that all directors have significant personal capital invested in Berkshire’s stock.) He believes directors should have exposure to the consequences of poor decisions (Berkshire does not carry insurance for its directors) and should not be reliant on the income from board fees, which are minimal at Berkshire.”
on Warren Buffett

Buffett’s view of governance is opposite to most public-company orthodoxy. Small board, owners not professionals, no insurance, minimal fees. The result is a group whose interests are aligned with shareholders by structure, not by exhortation.

For a private holdco, the equivalent is to build an advisory or operating board of people who have personally allocated capital, run companies, and felt the downside of bad decisions. Pay in equity or carry where possible. Avoid professional directors who serve on too many boards to take ownership of any one of them.

The holdco’s job is to compound capital. The board exists to challenge and protect that work. A board of owners does it. A board of resume-builders does not.

Lesson 12
Avoid the Edifice Complex
“There is an apparent inverse correlation between the construction of elaborate new headquarters buildings and investor returns. In contrast, not one of the outsider CEOs built lavish headquarters.”
The Outsiders

The Edifice Complex is a real signal. Companies that build trophy headquarters underperform. Capital Cities operated out of a converted convent. Buffett still works in the same office. Malone’s TCI was famous for its frugality. Smith ran General Cinema with a four-person Office of the Chairman.

For a holdco, the pattern matters because it sets the tone for the operating companies. If corporate is spending on its own comfort, every operating company manager learns that the rules at the center are different from the rules in the field. That is poison for a decentralized model.

Frugality at HQ is not about saving money. The amounts involved are small. It is about signaling, every day, that the holdco serves the operating companies and the shareholders, not the other way around.
